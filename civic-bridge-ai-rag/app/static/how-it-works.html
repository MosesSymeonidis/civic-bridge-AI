It is not a keyword filter. It is a guided reasoning system.

The chatbot hides a deliberately layered method: legal and policy standards ground the answer, semantic-barrier theory explains the communicative harm, and pedagogy turns the analysis into classroom action.

Grounded standards

Council of Europe risk signals

Civic Bridge AI uses a four-tier severity model grounded in Council of Europe Recommendation CM/Rec(2022)16. It distinguishes protected offensive expression from denigration, dehumanisation, and incitement risk.

Semantic barriers

What the message does to dialogue

The system looks for the seven codebook mechanisms: rigid opposition, transfer of meaning, prohibited thoughts, stigma, undermining the motive, distrust, and bracketing. That shifts the focus from labels to repairable dialogue failures.

Practical guidance

From risk signal to next step

The assistant offers a controlled path: understand the incident, teach with it, or report it. It keeps legal determination with human authorities and keeps educators focused on safe, proportionate action.

Roles and age groups shape the conversation.

A teacher needs practical classroom moves. A student needs a thinking partner. A younger learner needs safer wording. The same incident is therefore handled differently depending on who is asking.

T

Teacher guidance

Teachers receive direct explanations, classroom options, RFCDC links, and structured choices: understand, teach, or report. The assistant avoids overloading the teacher with a full lesson plan until they ask for depth.

S

Student guidance

Students get Socratic support: who is targeted, whose voice is missing, and what the message asks people to feel. If the student appears personally targeted, safety and help-seeking come first.

And if the student is the target?

If a student writes "they keep posting things about me and I am scared", the analysis never runs. A rule-based screen, no AI involved, answers instantly with help-seeking first: talk to a trusted adult, the country's child helpline (116 111 in Cyprus), evidence preservation, and a gentle way back if the system misread the situation. This is what the system refuses to do: it will not give a scared child a lecture on discourse analysis.

The teaching theory is dialogue restoration.

The goal is not only to classify a harmful message. The goal is to help teachers and students understand how speech closes democratic conversation, then practise ways to reopen it.

Notice the target and frame.
Who is being spoken about, and as what kind of person or group?
Name the semantic barrier.
Identify the mechanism: rigid opposition, transfer of meaning, stigma, distrust, prohibited thoughts, undermining the motive, or bracketing.
Connect to RFCDC competences.
Use democratic culture competences such as empathy, critical thinking, respect, and tolerance of ambiguity.
Choose a safe response path.
Classroom activity, supportive conversation, counter-speech, documentation, or country-specific reporting.

Seven semantic barriers explain why some speech breaks civic dialogue.

Hate speech is not only a set of bad words. It works through specific, recognisable mechanisms that shut down dialogue. The codebook encodes seven of them from peer-reviewed social psychology (Moscovici; Gillespie 2008; Avraamidou and Psaltis 2019), each with linguistic markers, annotated examples, and matched counter-strategies.

Rigid oppositionBinary us-versus-them framing that leaves no middle ground: "they will never change".
Transfer of meaningLinking a group to an already loaded category (invasion, disease, foreign agents) so hostility transfers automatically.
Prohibited thoughtsMarking the alternative view as dangerous even to consider: "it would be the end of us".
StigmaBranding those who engage with the other side as traitors or naive, enforcing conformity.
Undermining the motiveDismissing a speaker by attacking who pays or drives them instead of what they say.
DistrustCategorical, history-laden distrust of an entire group as a source: "you cannot trust a single one".
BracketingHolding the other view at a distance through hedging or refusal: "their version of history", "nothing to discuss".

From single incidents to the aggregate picture.

Every analysis contributes categorical signals (tier, mechanisms, themes) to an aggregate view. Never the text, never an identity. The awareness dashboard turns the stream of incidents into something a municipality, ministry, or school authority can act on: where education is the right tool, where reporting channels need strengthening, and which dialogue-blocking mechanisms dominate local discourse.

This is the surface for policy makers and institutions. They do not chat with the assistant; they read the aggregate picture, never individuals, framed through the graduated-response logic of CM/Rec(2022)16.

What the jury does not see in the chat window.

The visible chatbot is the last step. Behind it is a set of constraints that keep the answer grounded, age-aware, educational, and safe.

Analysis card

The tier card is generated separately from the conversational reply. It shows risk signals for human review while the assistant keeps the conversation focused on next steps.

Retrieval with citations

Open questions trigger retrieval from the legal corpus. The interface collapses sources by default, but every reference can be opened.

Reporting support

Reporting contacts appear when escalation is chosen, including country-specific reporting options such as Cyprus cybercrime, helplines, and equality-body information.

Safeguards are part of the product, not fine print.

The system is deliberately constrained because the domain is sensitive: children, schools, discrimination, and possible legal escalation.

No legal determinations.
The system describes risk signals; authorities and courts decide legality.
Human review by design.
The output supports educators and reviewers, not automated enforcement.
Privacy-first prompts.
It avoids usernames, school names, profile links, exact locations, and names of minors.
Safety triage for students.
If a student may be personally targeted, support and help-seeking take priority.

A bridge between standards, classrooms, and action.

Civic Bridge AI makes the reasoning behind hate-speech guidance visible: standards for legitimacy, semantic barriers for understanding, pedagogy for repair, and country contacts for real-world response.
